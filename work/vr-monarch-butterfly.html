VR Monarch Butterfly

An immersive VR education experience tracing the monarch butterfly migration through embodied perspective-taking — designed to build environmental empathy.

Overview

Monarch butterfly populations have declined by over 80% in the past two decades. Despite the scale of the ecological crisis, public awareness and behavioral change have been slow. Abstract statistics don't move people; lived experience might.

This project, developed in Cornell's HCI research lab, investigated whether embodied VR perspective-taking (becoming a monarch butterfly, not just observing one) could measurably increase environmental concern and pro-conservation behavior intentions.

Problem

Environmental education typically operates at two extremes: dry factual content (ineffective at motivating behavior change) or emotionally manipulative imagery (creates distress rather than agency). We needed a third path: experiential learning that is emotionally engaging without being psychologically destabilizing.

  • VR presence: How do you create genuine sense of embodiment in a non-human form, within current hardware constraints?
  • Educational accuracy: VR encourages simplification; the monarch's biology and migration are complex and important to represent accurately.
  • Comfort and accessibility: Motion sickness is a major barrier in ecological VR. Design must prioritize comfort without sacrificing immersion.

Process

Literature Review

We reviewed 40+ papers on VR-based empathy, environmental education, and non-human embodiment. Key finding: studies using "first-person embodiment" (you are the entity) showed significantly stronger behavioral intention changes than "third-person observation" (you watch the entity).

Ecological Consultation

Partnered with Cornell's Entomology department to ensure biological accuracy. Key design constraint: monarch butterflies navigate using magnetic field sensing and sun position — two things that don't translate intuitively to game controllers. We spent three design iterations solving this locomotion problem.

Iterative VR Prototyping

Five prototype iterations over 6 months. Key design decisions were resolved through A/B testing with 30 participants:

  • First-person perspective consistently outperformed third-person for empathy measures
  • Thermals (warm air updrafts) as the primary locomotion mechanic — more biologically accurate and significantly reduced motion sickness vs. joystick flight
  • Sparse UI, diegetic narration — information delivered as "inner experience" rather than HUD overlay
Prototype 3 — locomotion mechanic testing session

Solution

Embodied Migration Journey

The 15-minute experience follows a monarch butterfly's 3,000-mile migration from central Canada to the Oyamel forests of central Mexico. Players "become" the butterfly — sensing thermals, navigating by the sun, stopping to feed at milkweed patches, and experiencing the threats (pesticides, habitat loss, climate disruption) that have decimated the population.

Thermal Locomotion System

Rather than joystick-based flight (which causes motion sickness and feels un-butterfly-like), we developed a thermal navigation system: players tilt and orient their bodies to find warm air currents, which lift and propel them. It's slower than conventional VR movement, but creates a meditative, genuinely embodied sensation of soaring.

Ecological Threat Sequences

Three scripted "threat sequences" — pesticide exposure (sensory disruption effects), habitat fragmentation (milkweed patches replaced by farmland), and climate delay (late autumn temperatures disrupting navigation cues) — convey the ecological pressures through direct experience rather than statistics.

Reflection

The thermal locomotion system was the most important design decision of the project — and the one we almost didn't make. Every conventional VR precedent pointed toward joystick flight. The thermal system required building a custom physics simulation, and it took until the third prototype for the team to fully believe it would work.

The lesson: the most authentic design choice and the most comfortable engineering choice are often different. Authentic design is usually worth the engineering cost.

I'd want to extend the experience to 30 minutes and add a "post-migration reflection" segment — a quiet moment at the Oyamel forest where players can look back on their journey. That emotional landing feels important and we ran out of time to build it properly.
