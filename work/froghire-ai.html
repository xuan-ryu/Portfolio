Overview

FrogHire.ai uses machine learning to help companies identify strong candidates beyond traditional resumes. My work focused on redesigning the candidate-facing experience — specifically the screening and skills-assessment flow — making the AI evaluation process feel fair, transparent, and genuinely human.

The platform serves early-career candidates (0–3 years experience) who are most vulnerable to opaque algorithmic filtering. Getting this wrong means qualified people never get a fair chance.

Overview — candidate flow diagram

Problem

Early-career candidates reported feeling alienated by black-box AI screening systems. The existing interface failed to communicate what the AI was evaluating, leaving users anxious and disempowered. Three core pain points emerged from initial discovery research:

  • Opacity: Candidates had no idea what skills or behaviors the AI was measuring during live assessments.
  • Perceived unfairness: Without context, users assumed bias — especially candidates from non-traditional backgrounds.
  • Lack of feedback: The old system provided zero signal after completion. Candidates felt like they shouted into a void.

Internally, the platform also struggled with high candidate dropout rates (71%) during the screening phase — a direct business problem that aligned with the user experience problem.

Process

Research

I conducted 12 semi-structured user interviews with recent graduates and early-career job seekers across different industries. Sessions were 45–60 minutes and covered past experiences with automated hiring tools, mental models of "fairness," and emotional responses to uncertainty.

Affinity mapping of interview transcripts surfaced three clusters: control, legibility, and closure. Candidates didn't just want a fair process — they wanted to understand it, feel capable within it, and receive acknowledgment afterward.

Competitive Analysis

I reviewed 8 competing hiring platforms (including HireVue, Pymetrics, and LinkedIn's Skills Assessments). Most fell into one of two failure modes: over-explaining upfront (front-loading anxiety) or explaining nothing at all. None provided contextual, in-the-moment guidance.

Design Sprints

Three 2-week design sprints, each ending with moderated usability testing. Sprint 1 focused on the onboarding and framing; Sprint 2 on the live assessment interface; Sprint 3 on the post-submission feedback layer.

Sprint 2 — assessment interface wireframes

45 participants tested across the two major rounds of testing (Sprint 2 + 3). Testing was conducted via Maze for unmoderated click-through tests and Zoom for moderated sessions.

Solution

A redesigned onboarding and evaluation flow built around three principles: Explain before, encourage during, affirm after.

Transparent Framing

Before the assessment begins, a plain-language brief explains exactly what FrogHire measures (problem-solving approach, communication clarity, adaptability) and — crucially — what it does not measure (accent, appearance, demographic signals). This reframes the experience from "test" to "conversation."

Contextual Micro-guidance

During live tasks, small contextual cues ("Take your time — there's no single correct answer") reduced anxiety without interrupting flow. These cues are triggered by hesitation patterns, not timers, so they feel responsive rather than patronizing.

Strengths-based Feedback

Instead of a binary pass/fail or an opaque score, candidates receive a "Strengths Summary" 24 hours after submission — three specific observations written in plain language. Even candidates who don't advance get meaningful signal about what they demonstrated well.

The redesign shipped to a beta cohort of 1,200 candidates in Q1 2024. The dropout reduction translated to a meaningful increase in completed assessments for hiring partners — directly addressing the business case for the project.

Reflection

The biggest design lesson: transparency isn't enough — timing matters. My first instinct was to front-load explanations during onboarding. Testing quickly revealed that candidates ignored them; anxiety was lowest when the same information appeared in context, precisely when it was relevant.

I also underestimated how much the feedback layer would matter. Candidates who received the Strengths Summary — even those who didn't advance — were significantly more likely to use FrogHire again. Closure is undervalued in hiring UX.

If I returned to this project, I'd invest more time in the employer-facing side: making it easier for hiring teams to understand and act on candidate strengths data, not just rank candidates.
