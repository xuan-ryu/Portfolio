A speculative design and research project exploring collective digital ownership, community governance, and the infrastructure commons in virtual space.

Overview

Digital infrastructure — search engines, social platforms, cloud storage — is overwhelmingly owned by a handful of corporations. The "digital commons" that early internet idealists imagined has largely been enclosed and commercialized. But what if it hadn't been? What if we designed it differently now?

Digital Socialism Domain is a speculative design project that prototypes what collectively-owned digital infrastructure might look and feel like — and, critically, what governance mechanisms would need to exist to make it genuinely democratic rather than simply decentralized.

Problem

Decentralized web projects (Mastodon, Fediverse, etc.) have largely failed to achieve mainstream adoption. The research question driving this project: is the barrier primarily technical, primarily cultural, or primarily a design problem?

  • Governance is invisible: Most decentralized platforms make governance mechanisms technically available but socially invisible. Most users have no idea how decisions get made.
  • Ownership without agency: "You own your data" is not a meaningful statement if users have no mechanism to participate in how the platform operates.
  • The commons dilemma: Without clear governance, shared resources degrade. Decentralized ≠ governed by commons logic.

Process

Theoretical Foundation

The project draws on Elinor Ostrom's work on commons governance, P2P Foundation research on platform cooperativism, and case studies of successful digital cooperatives (Stocksy, Resonate, CoopCycle). The design is grounded in: not how to decentralize, but how to govern.

Community Research

In-depth interviews with 15 participants across three communities: Mastodon instance administrators, worker cooperative members, and open-source maintainers. All three groups had direct experience managing shared resources — and remarkably consistent insights about what makes governance work or fail.

Speculative Design Workshops

Three participatory design workshops with 8–12 participants each. Using "design fiction" methods, participants co-designed governance interfaces, ownership visualization systems, and community decision-making flows. The goal was not to prototype a product but to surface the design principles that would make collective ownership legible and actionable.

Workshop 2 — governance interface co-design artifacts

Solution

The Commons Dashboard

A design prototype for what a "commons dashboard" might look like — a living interface that makes collective ownership tangible. It shows: what shared infrastructure you collectively own, how decisions affecting it are made, your participation history, and the resource health of the commons you belong to.

Governance Legibility Patterns

A pattern library of 12 interface patterns for making governance mechanisms legible: transparent voting interfaces, decision impact visualization, "governance audit trails," and minority voice amplification patterns. These are documented as reusable components for platform cooperatives building governance UIs.

The Research Document

A 40-page research document synthesizing findings into actionable design principles for digital commons infrastructure. Circulated to relevant communities via the P2P Foundation and DisCO Collective.

Reflection

This project was the furthest I've worked from conventional product design — and the most intellectually formative. The core insight: design is politics. Every interface choice embeds assumptions about who has power, who makes decisions, and who the system is for.

The governance legibility patterns turned out to be the most practically useful output. Organizations working on digital commons don't need more philosophy — they need patterns they can actually implement. Meeting practitioners where they are is more useful than producing perfect theoretical frameworks.

What I'd do differently: start with the practitioner interviews earlier. The theoretical framing I brought in from the literature review turned out to be less useful than the lived wisdom of people who've actually managed commons at scale. Theory should follow practice, not precede it.
